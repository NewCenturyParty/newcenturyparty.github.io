The Problem

Food is becoming more expensive, harder to secure, harder to produce, and no one but Bay Street sees any benefit.

The Promise

The NCP will restore power to the farmer, restore food security to the consumer, and ensure the breadbasket of Canada lives up to its name and promise of feeding its people.

The Plan

Within 12 months of forming government, the NCP will introduce the following bills:

  • The Saskatchewan Agriculture Board Act.
  • The Saskatchewan Grocery Corporation Act.
  • The Saskatchewan Farmer Right to Repair Act.

These bills will ensure that Saskatchewan is the best place in the world to produce and procure food.

The Saskatchewan Agriculture Board:

  • The Saskatchewan Agriculture Board (SAB) will be mandated to ensure Saskatchewan farmers own the value chain from seed to sale, domestic food security, and that Saskatchewan families are fed by Saskatchewan soil.
  • Revenue Protection:
    • Farmers may sign up to pay into a voluntary margin insurance program that guarantees a floor price based on the Regional Benchmark Cost of Production (RBCP) plus a cost of living margin. This will act as a backstop against global market fluctuations.
    • Premiums will be cost shared with the Province.
    • If the average market price drops below the RBCP plus the cost of living margin the SAB pays the difference.
  • Voluntary Pools:
    • The SAB will operate voluntary pools for all major commodities, including grains, pulses, and produce.
    • Farmers who commit to a pool before Spring will receive an immediate, interest free, Spring Time Advance (STA) of up to 75% of the projected floor price upfront. The STA will be secured against the crop and integrated with the Saskatchewan Crop Insurance Corporation (SCIC) to cover any weather risks. No more high-interest operating loans.
    • SAB operates at-cost. Meaning, after hedging and sales are complete, 100% of net profit from the pool is returned to the producers as a final payment.
  • Saskatchewan Value Chains:
    • SaskGrocery and local independent processors shall have the Right of First Refusal to purchase any commodity from the SAB pools before export markets.
    • The SAB will partner with the Saskatchewan Manufacturing Corporation (SMC) to build and operate regional flour mills, crushers, and abattoirs to ensure raw products are processed into food inside Saskatchewan by Saskatchewan workers.
      • See more on the SMC in Policy 19 - Manufacturing.
  • Bulk Procurement:
    • The SAB will operate a Bulk Procurement Division, negotiating directly with global manufacturers for fertilizer fuel, and chemistry for all members of SAB pools.
    • These products will be sold to members of SAB pools at Wholesale Costs + Logistics.
  • Grading Rights:
    • The SAB will operate a Grading Authority Division, providing arbitration for grading disputes.
    • Any producer delivering to any elevator in Saskatchewan (public or private) has the absolute right to contest the grade, dockage, or moisture content assigned by the buyer.
    • Upon contest, at no cost to the producer, a sealed sample is sent to the SAB Grading Authority Division who will deliver a final and binding grade.
    • If the buyer is found to have downgraded the grain incorrectly, the buyer pays all testing fees and an administrative penalty to the producer.
  • Logistics and Infrastructure:
    • The SAB will acquire and maintain a fleet of SAB owned rail hopper cars to ensure grain moves to market efficiently.
    • The SAB will invest in strategic networks of Inland Terminals and Value-Add Processing Plants within Saskatchewan.
    • SAB Terminals will prioritize selling grains to SaskGrocery and local processors before exporting raw commodities, keeping the value add at home.
  • Regenerative Dividend:
    • A per-acre annual dividend paid to farmers who maintain certified soil-health practices such as cover crops, diverse rotations, shelter belts, and more.
    • Farmers are the protectors and stewards of much of Saskatchewan land and it is time we pay them for the ecosystem services they provide along side the food production.
    • Compliance will be determined by random audits and remote sensing.
  • Farmland Trust:
    • The SAB Farmland Trust will be established and will have the Right of First Refusal on any farmland being sold in Saskatchewan, excluding transfers to immediate family members.
    • Land acquired by the Trust will be offered as Lease-to-Own agreements only to Saskatchewan residents who commit to farming the land with local ownership, either as an individual or as a co-op.
    • No more Bay Street farms.
  • Protections:
    • All members of the SAB are protected under The Saskatchewan Farm Security Act.
    • Any violations of the Act will be met with stiff fines and potential loss of business licenses.
    • Supplier retaliation prohibited for selling to or working with SAB / SaskGrocery.
    • No sale of any customer, producer, supplier, or consumer data whatsoever.

SaskGrocery:

  • The Saskatchewan Grocery Corporation (SaskGrocery) is a new Crown corporation with a mandate of eliminating food deserts, food insecurity, and must operate on a break-even basis, prioritizing the lowest possible prices and fair labour standards over profit accumulation, and ensuring the people of Saskatchewan can cheaply and easily get the groceries they need wherever they live.
  • SaskGrocery will be made of two entities: SaskGrocery Wholesale, and SaskGrocery retail.
  • SaskGrocery Wholesale:
    • A provincial public wholesaler, purchasing massive local and local bulk to secure the lowest possible unit costs.
    • Mandated to prioritize purchasing from the Saskatchewan Agriculture Board (SAB) and local processors and producers.
    • SaskGrocery Wholesale will sell to any Saskatchewan-based independent grocer, Co-op, or northern / rural store front at the same landed price as it sells to its own SaskGrocery Storefronts.
  • SaskGrocery Retail:
    • SaskGrocery retail stores will come in these formats:
      • SaskGrocery Super: Full-service centres in major hubs.
      • SaskGrocery Market: Mid-size locations for neighbourhoods and towns of over 1000 people.
      • SaskGrocery Mini: Converted convenience-style footprints for downtowns and food deserts.
      • SaskGrocery Mobile: Integrated with the Saskatchewan Transport Corporation to deliver online ordered groceries to secure, cold storage lockers at STC depots.
        • See more about the STC in Policy 06 - 21st Century Connected Communities.
    • Operations and Logistics:
      • The STC will be the main logistics provider for SaskGrocery, delivering food in cold storage secured busses daily, as well as weekly freight trucks for the bulk of supplies.
    • Labour Standards:
      • All employees must be unionized either by the SGEU or a new local, and must be paid and scheduled in accordance with Policy 09 - Fair Labour.
      • Stores will utilize high-efficiency stocking methods like pallet to floor, and simplified inventories with less brands but best prices.
    • Waste Prevention:
      • Unsold edible food must be donated to local food banks.
      • Good-Samaritan liability shields will be in place for compliant donors who comply with temperature, handling, and recall protocols.
      • HACCP/Cold-Chain record keeping (temp logs, recalls) will be required for donations.
      • If food cannot be donated it must be composted, and such compost can be sold on site to customers or to SAB for soil health programs.
    • The Pricing Model:
      • There will be no slotting fees.
      • The Essential Basket:
        • A designated list of staple items will be sold at landed cost + 1%.
          • Basic Basket items would include things such as: bread, eggs, milk, flour, produce, diapers, formula, etc.
        • Essential Basket items will be loss leaders, subsidized by the sale of non-essentials.
      • General Merchandising:
        • All other items will be priced via a Sustainability Formula:
          • Price = (Landed Cost of Goods) + (Share of Operating Expenses like wages, power, rent) + (2% Reserve).
      • Transparency:
        • SaskGrocery will publish a monthly Basket Index, showing the average costs of its Essentials Basket compared to private competitors.
        • The list of Essential Basket items will be posted publicly, and reviewed annually to ensure the prices and items meet the needs of Saskatchewan residents.
      • Geographic Parity:
        • Essential Basket Items will cost the same in places like La Loche as it does in Regina or Saskatoon, because geography should not be a poverty tax.

The Saskatchewan Farmer Right to Repair Act:

  • Farmers will have the absolute right to diagnose, maintain, and repair their own equipment, or allow 3rd parties to do the same services.
  • All agricultural equipment manufacturers selling in Saskatchewan must provide farmers and independent repair shops with:
    • Diagnostic software and tools.
    • Service manuals and calibration files.
    • Replacement parts at a fair market value.
    • The ability to reset immobilizers or digital locks after a repair.
  • Access must be provided on Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory (FRAND) terms.
    • This means no subscription fees for diagnostic tools that exceed the cost of providing the tool.
    • No delaying parts delivery to force a dealer repair.
  • Any sales contract or End User License Agreement (EULA) that waives the buyer's right to repair or claims that the manufacturer retains ownership of the software or firmware is void and unenforceable in Saskatchewan.
  • Enforcement:
    • Failure to comply results in a fine of $25,000 per day per model.
    • The Minister may ban the sale of non-compliant equipment in the province if the manufacturer refuses to cooperate.
