The Fiscal Philosophy

FIVE PRINCIPLES

We all pay our fair share to share in a fair society.

Principle 01

Honesty

The public deserves to see every assumption, every risk, and every trade-off. This framework uses low, base, and high scenarios because no honest 25-year model produces one exact number.

Principle 02

Investment, Not Expense

More than half of the platform's net cost sits in productive capital and public asset buildout: housing, utilities, factories, infrastructure, and resilience systems that last.

Principle 03

Value Retention

Money that stays and circulates in Saskatchewan generates more local wealth than money extracted to distant shareholders. The SFG, Crowns, credit unions, and public ownership structures are built to reduce leakage.

Principle 04

Intergenerational Equity

The Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund preserves resource wealth for future generations, and long-lived capital projects are financed across the decades of benefit they deliver.

Principle 05

Fiscal Sustainability

The peak year is not the permanent year. The platform is front-loaded in cost and back-loaded in return, with capital intensity falling and recovery channels deepening over time.

How It Is Funded

Funding Lanes

Because the platform is not one kind of spending, it cannot be funded through one kind of revenue. Policy 21 uses separate funding lanes matched to the fiscal character of what they fund.

Core GRF and Tax-Supported State

Income security, health operations, education operations, public administration, labour enforcement, democracy, reconciliation, and safety.

Dedicated Levies and Earmarked Revenue

The Universal Health Levy, transition levies, resource royalties, windfall levies, dormant lease taxes, and value-add levies.

Crown and Utility Borrowing

Thermal networks, utility systems, grid expansion, and parts of digital infrastructure financed with rate-backed or service-backed recovery.

SaskBonds and Long-Dated Capital

Public housing, factories, resilience retrofits, grocery and logistics assets, and other long-lived public capital.

Enterprise Revenues and User Recoveries

Rents, thermal-service charges, contract revenue, vendor savings, utility revenue, and public-enterprise sales that lower long-run net cost.

Intergenerational Wealth Architecture

The SSF and SFG are balance-sheet architecture, not ordinary day-to-day program financing.

The current base case does not hard-code a full province-wide tax reform package. Core operating functions are financed through Saskatchewan's ordinary tax base, federal transfers, and annual budget decisions taken within Policy 21's fiscal guardrails.

The Capital Budget

SaskBonds and Productive Debt

The capital budget should be treated separately from the operating budget. Long-lived public assets should be financed on long horizons that match the duration of the assets they create.

This is the logic of productive debt. Housing stock, thermal systems, grid assets, resilience retrofits, digital infrastructure, factories, and logistics systems are not routine operating deficits. They are assets that lower future costs, create recoverable revenue, or increase the province's durable productive capacity.

Secondary Returns

What The Model Does Not Overclaim

Policy 21 separates hard offsets from slower feedback effects. Poverty reduction, preventive care, stronger education, lower household costs, and local value capture all matter. They just do not all belong in the base case. However, they do present a case that there is more upside to be had, should the plan work.

Household Cost Compression

Social Security, public housing, connected communities, universal health, food sovereignty, and fair labour lower the unavoidable monthly costs crushing households.

Saskatchewan Value Capture

Co-operatives, food sovereignty, resource and energy sovereignty, digital sovereignty, financial sovereignty, and manufacturing reduce leakage by keeping ownership, margins, and procurement inside Saskatchewan.

Upside Not Required

Climate lawsuit winnings, stronger migration, stronger-than-modeled power and resource revenues, and larger productivity gains are treated as upside, not as the core funding case.

Guardrails

Fiscal Safeguards

The platform is large, ambitious, and long-running. It needs rules that prevent drift, overclaiming, and fiscal disorder.

1

Climate Lawsuit Independence

The platform does not rely on climate lawsuit winnings to make the core math work.

2

SSF Supermajority Lock

The SSF principal cannot be accessed and the disbursement formula cannot change without a two-thirds supermajority vote.

3

METR Cap

Combined marginal effective tax rates must not exceed 55% at any income level. Annual publication is required.

4

Bond Transparency

Every SaskBond series must publish annual allocation reports showing exactly where every dollar was spent.

5

Staged Approval of Major Capital

Reactors, major grid works, factory buildouts, and other large projects proceed through decision gates instead of being treated as all-or-nothing instant obligations.

6

Separate Reporting

GRF expansion, Crown capital, utility borrowing, rate-backed recovery, and intergenerational wealth architecture must always be reported separately.

Questions & Answers

THE HARD QUESTIONS

How can you afford all of this?

The same way every major economy has ever built transformative infrastructure: you invest public money in productive assets, you capture a fairer share of the wealth already produced in the province, and you finance long-lived capital over long horizons. This is a 25-year restructuring program, not a one-year budget trick.

Won’t this drive businesses out of Saskatchewan?

We are building a healthier, better-educated, better-housed workforce, connected by stronger infrastructure, powered by abundant energy, and backed by public and local financial institutions that keep more capital circulating here. Some employers will face higher costs in the short term, but the transition grants, stronger consumer demand, and improved infrastructure are designed to make Saskatchewan a more attractive place to invest over time.

What if the Climate Lawsuit fails?

The entire fiscal model is built without a single dollar of lawsuit proceeds. If the lawsuit produces nothing, nothing changes. If it produces anything, it is all upside flowing directly to the SSF.

Isn’t all this bond debt too much?

Not all debt is the same. Borrowing to cover routine shortfalls with no structural reform is very different from borrowing to build homes, utilities, factories, and infrastructure that last for decades. Policy 21 treats productive debt and ordinary operating pressure separately for exactly that reason.

Does the platform balance within 25 years?

Not on the hard base case. Under the debt-service model, annual balance returns around 2053 on the moderate balance path and around 2042 on the transformation path. The platform is not sold as instantly self-financing. It is sold as a long-horizon reconstruction program with a plausible route back to balance if the broader economic thesis works.
