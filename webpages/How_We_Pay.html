The Fiscal Philosophy

FIVE PRINCIPLES

We all pay our fair share to share in a fair society.

Principle 01

Honesty

The public deserves to see every assumption, every risk, and every trade-off. This document hides nothing. Every projection is stated. Every gap is acknowledged.

Principle 02

Investment, Not Expense

The vast majority of new spending creates productive assets, builds human capital, or generates revenue that compounds over time. We are planting trees whose shade we will sit in.

Principle 03

Velocity of Money

Money that stays and circulates in Saskatchewan generates more wealth than money extracted to distant shareholders. The SFG, credit unions, Crown Corporations, and SaskBonds keep the circular flow of dollars within our borders.

Principle 04

Intergenerational Equity

The Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund ensures resource wealth is saved and invested for future generations, not consumed today. Bond-funded capital projects spread costs across the decades of benefit they deliver.

Principle 05

Fiscal Sustainability

The platform phases in over 4–8 years. Early deficits are structural investments. By Year 10–12, Crown Corporations generate returns and the SSF contributes meaningfully. By Year 15–20, the system approaches dynamic fiscal balance.

How We Pay

Revenue Pillars

Six interlocking revenue streams fund the Saskatchewan 21 platform. Together they grow total provincial revenue from ~$20 billion today to ~$41 billion by Year 10 — and ~$58 billion by Year 20.

Progressive Taxation

+$1.6B/yr New Tax Revenue by Year 2

All personal income tax brackets rise by 1.5 percentage points, with two new high-income brackets at $200K+ and $500K+. The PST rises from 6% to 7%. Corporate rates increase 1–2 points for large firms only.

  • Small business rate protected at 1%
  • Co-operatives fully protected
  • Dignity Tax Credit offsets PIT for incomes under $50K
  • Priority Professional Tax Credits for nurses, teachers, physicians

Universal Health Levy

$2.1B/yr At Full Implementation

A new payroll levy replaces existing private health insurance premiums. The average worker and employer pay no more overall than they currently pay — the money is simply redirected from private insurers to the provincial system.

  • Revenue legally restricted to health spending only
  • Rate set by an independent arm’s-length board
  • Employers prohibited from clawing back through wage cuts
  • Grows to ~$3.5B/yr by Year 20

Resource Royalties

$3.3B → $9B/yr Year 1 to Year 20

The current opaque royalty regime is replaced with a simple, transparent two-tier system: a base rate targeting at least 35% of economic profit, and a windfall surcharge capturing up to 49% during boom periods.

  • All existing loopholes and royalty holidays closed
  • Windfall above 5-year rolling average → Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund
  • 15% of all base royalties flow directly to the SSF
  • Driven by uranium, lithium, potash, and rare earth expansion

Crown Corporation Dividends

$1.5B → $5.7B/yr Today to Year 20

Existing Crowns continue their dividends. As new Crowns mature — SaskGrocery, STC, SNRC, SMC, SDS, SFG — they transition from cost centres to profit centres, generating billions in new annual dividends.

  • Dividends above 5-year rolling average → SSF
  • SNRC projected to contribute $640M/yr by Year 10
  • SMC factory revenue offsets its costs by Year 10–12
  • By Year 20, new Crowns contribute ~$3B/yr

Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund

$47.7B Projected Principal by Year 20

Saskatchewan’s intergenerational endowment. Seeded at $500M, it grows through resource windfalls, excess Crown dividends, and the Energy Veterans Surcharge. Target return: 4–5% annually.

  • Generates $2B+/yr in interest by Year 20
  • 54% of disbursements fund the Dignity Dividend
  • 15% funds Reconciliation; 15% Resilience & Energy Veterans
  • Principal locked by two-thirds supermajority vote

SaskBonds

$77B Total Capital Program over 20 Years

Government obligation bonds fund productive capital: homes, energy grids, factories, and infrastructure. Every dollar is ring-fenced to a specific project. Assets generate revenue to service their own debt.

  • $100 minimum — any Saskatchewan resident can invest
  • SSF acts as first-loss protection for senior bond tranche
  • Six series: Housing, Energy, Infrastructure, Manufacturing, Foundation, Investment
  • Annual public allocation reports required for each series

How the Gap Is Managed

Provincial Borrowing

Operating deficits are financed at AA-rated GO bond rates (~3.5–4.0%). Saskatchewan’s combined debt-to-GDP ratio stays below 30% throughout the deficit years — well within the range of other Canadian provinces.

Economic Growth Feedback

Every 1% of additional GDP generates approximately $500M in additional tax revenue. The platform projects GDP 28% larger than the status quo baseline by Year 20 — already captured conservatively in the revenue projections above.

Crown Maturation

New Crowns transition from cost centres to profit centres between Years 8–15, eventually contributing $3B+ annually in dividends that generate no cost to government — because government owns the asset.

SSF Compounding

By Year 20, SSF interest alone covers $1.1B of the Dignity Dividend and $300M of reconciliation. By Year 30 — not modelled here — the SSF generates $3–4B annually, approaching full self-funding of those programs.

Climate Lawsuit Upside

Not one dollar of potential lawsuit proceeds is included in these projections. Any settlement or judgment — estimated $10–100B potential — flows directly to the SSF. It is pure upside contingency, deliberately excluded to keep the base case honest.

Guardrails

Fiscal Safeguards

Seven hard rules protect the fiscal system from political misuse. These are not aspirations — they are legislated constraints with defined consequences for breach.

1

METR Cap

Combined Marginal Effective Tax Rate must not exceed 55% at any income level. Annual public publication required. If breached, corrective measures must be tabled within 60 days of the budget.

2

SSF Supermajority Lock

The SSF principal cannot be accessed and the disbursement formula cannot change without a two-thirds supermajority vote in the Legislature. No future government can raid the fund.

3

Bond Transparency

Every SaskBond series must publish annual allocation reports showing exactly where every dollar was spent. Second-party opinions required. No general revenue slush — every dollar ring-fenced.

4

Crown Performance Reviews

Each new Crown must publish quarterly financial dashboards. Any Crown operating at a loss for more than five consecutive years must submit a restructuring plan to the Legislature for review.

5

Automatic Sunsets

SME Transition Grants (4 years), the Energy Veterans Surcharge (15 years), and all Oil & Gas operational licences (15 years) sunset automatically. No legislative action required to end temporary measures.

6

Debt Ceiling

Total provincial debt must not exceed 60% of GDP. If the ratio exceeds 50%, the Minister of Finance must table a fiscal consolidation plan within 90 days of the budget date.

7

Annual Full Disclosure

A full PBO-style fiscal update published every year: 20-year projections, METR analysis, Crown performance dashboards, SSF statements, and bond allocation reports. The numbers hide nothing.

Questions & Answers

THE HARD QUESTIONS

How can you afford all of this?

The same way every major economy has ever built transformative infrastructure: you invest public money in productive assets that grow the economy, and you capture a fair share of resource wealth. We raise revenue through progressive taxation, payroll levies that replace private insurance premiums, redesigned royalties, and bonds backed by the assets they build. Then the larger economy generates the tax revenue to sustain the programs. It’s a virtuous cycle — not a magic trick.

Won’t this drive businesses out of Saskatchewan?

We are building the best-educated, healthiest, most well-rested workforce in Canada, connected by world-class infrastructure, powered by cheap abundant energy, and backed by a public financial system that keeps capital circulating locally. Businesses don’t flee quality — they flock to it. The 30-hour week and higher minimum wage raise short-term costs for some employers, but transition grants, expanded consumer spending, and access to SaskJobs-placed talent more than compensate.

What if the Climate Lawsuit fails?

The entire fiscal model is built without a single dollar of lawsuit proceeds. If the lawsuit produces nothing, nothing changes in the projections above. If it produces anything — and early constitutional analysis suggests the Redwater Strategy is well-grounded in provincial jurisdiction over environmental regulation — every dollar flows directly to the SSF as pure upside.

Isn’t $77 billion in bonds too much debt?

Saskatchewan’s total debt-to-GDP ratio stays below Ontario’s and Quebec’s throughout the entire 20-year period. And unlike consumption debt, this debt builds homes that generate rent, factories that generate revenue, nuclear reactors that generate electricity, and infrastructure that supports economic growth. A mortgage on a house you own is not the same as a credit card balance for a vacation. The bonds pay for themselves through the assets they create.

What if resource prices crash?

The SSF has a Stabilization Reserve equal to two years of expected disbursements. Royalty revenue flowing to the GRF is smoothed via a five-year rolling average, insulating the operating budget from boom-bust cycles. And the entire point of diversifying from oil and gas to uranium, lithium, potash, and rare earths is to reduce commodity concentration risk — no single commodity crash can destabilize the system.

What does the cumulative deficit actually add up to?

The cumulative operating deficit over 20 years is approximately $53 billion — financed through borrowing at AA-rated bond rates. For context, this builds 100,000 homes, universal health care, the world’s best K–12 school funding per student, a sovereign wealth fund that will outlast us all, and the energy infrastructure to power Saskatchewan for a century. Saskatchewan’s combined debt-to-GDP ratio remains below 30% throughout — lower than the Canadian federal government’s current ratio of 42%.
