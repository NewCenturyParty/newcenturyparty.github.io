The Xanatos Gambit

A Xanatos Gambit is a plan where every possible outcome benefits the planner. If opponents resist, they lose. If they comply, you win. If they do nothing, you still advance.

The Saskatchewan 21 Policies are not twenty-one separate ideas. They are one interlocking system designed so that every response by every actor — pay or don’t pay, cooperate or resist, stay or leave — advances the transformation of Saskatchewan. Every fork in the road leads to the same destination.

Policy 01

THE FOUNDATION: HUMAN DIGNITY

It starts here. The Saskatchewan Human Dignity Act establishes that the primary fiduciary duty of the Government of Saskatchewan is to respect, protect, and uphold Human Dignity. All other enactments must be read consistently with this Act.

This is not symbolic. It is the legal backbone of the entire platform. Every policy that follows — housing, food, energy, labour, reconciliation — flows from this duty. If the Government has a duty to uphold Human Dignity, then it has a duty to act on the rest of the S21P.

You cannot promise dignity and then refuse to deliver it.

The Opening Move

The Redwater Strategy

Immediately upon forming government, the Minister of Environment updates the Liability Management Ratio (LMR) to 2.0 for all oil and gas companies operating in Saskatchewan. Their assets must be worth at least double their environmental liabilities — the abandoned wells, the aging pipelines, the tailings ponds, all the mess they have left behind.

The difference between their current actual LMR and the new 2.0 standard must be posted as a cash bond. No credit. No corporate promises. Cold, hard, cash. Twenty-five percent within 90 days. The remainder within 12 months.

This creates the first fork.

Fork A: They Pay

If the oil companies post their bonds, that is potentially tens to hundreds of billions of dollars placed in trust for environmental remediation. That trust is held in the Saskatchewan Financial Group, where it accrues interest for the province before being used for cleanup. The interest income flows to the Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund.

Saskatchewan gets funded remediation and a massive injection of capital into its own financial system. The companies continue operating under new, stricter rules. Win.

Fork B: They Refuse

If a company cannot or refuses to post the bond, the regulator takes that as an admission that they cannot meet their statutory environmental obligations. They are, in the eyes of the law, insolvent with respect to their cleanup liabilities.

Their operating licenses are suspended. The assets are placed under provincial receivership through the Saskatchewan Natural Resources Corporation (SNRC) as operator of last resort. Win.

The Monday Morning Problem

To avoid any gap in energy supply, the Province offers brand new operating licenses back to the oil majors. But with conditions:

  • Begin cleaning up orphaned wells immediately
  • Pay the Energy Veterans Surcharge (5.5% on Saskatchewan revenues)
  • Clean up as they go — the land should be so clean their children could farm it
  • Agree to a 15-year sunset on all oil and gas operations, with extraction permits declining 10% annually for years 1–10 and 20% annually for years 11–15

If they refuse, they are the ones who have to explain to the White House and the American Mid-West why gas prices are about to triple. Either way, the province wins.

Policy 04

The Indigenous Shield

This is not a white boy telling Treaty Nations what to do. This is an invitation to co-govern. Not consult. Not advise. Co-govern. Two Sovereigns working together as equals.

The Consent Protocol establishes that before any project, policy, or action that affects Indigenous lands, communities, waters, heritage, culture, language, or livelihoods, the relevant Indigenous governing body must give Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC), as per UNDRIP. This must include cost sharing, revenue sharing, job opportunities, and capacity funding.

The Trap

If the Government of Canada, or any international body, tries to stop the S21P, they are not just going up against a provincial government. They are going up against Indigenous Title. They are going up against FPIC. They are going up against UNDRIP, which Canada itself has adopted.

The Canadian government would have to argue against Indigenous self-determination to stop Saskatchewan’s platform. That is politically, legally, and internationally untenable. Checkmate.

The Benefit

This partnership eliminates two of the biggest risks in resource development: protest risk and lawsuit risk. When Indigenous Nations are co-owners and co-governors with veto power, there is no one left to protest and no one left to sue.

That means more certainty, faster timelines, and lower costs for everyone. Security and confidence, built into the foundation.

Policy 15

THE RESOURCE LOCK

All new or expanded major resource projects in Saskatchewan must be trilaterally co-owned.

33%

Government of Saskatchewan

With veto power. The public interest is always at the table.

33%

Treaty Nations

With veto power. The original stewards share in every decision and every dollar.

33%

Private Corporation

Without veto power. Valued partners, but voluntary ones. If they refuse, their share reverts 50/50 to the Province and the Sovereigns.

Yes, it is a 66% haircut to the corporations. But it also saves millions in litigation and protest delays, and delivers an immediate 66% return on initial exploration CAPEX. If we all respect each other, things happen quickly.

The Vertical

Uranium OPEC

Saskatchewan has the highest-grade uranium deposits in the world. Under the current model, we ship raw yellowcake — uranium dirt that is literally yellow — out of the province and buy back the processed fuel. No more.

Policy 15 introduces a Value-Add Levy that makes raw export economically uncompetitive with domestic processing. The SNRC and the Saskatchewan Manufacturing Corporation will build a uranium conversion facility and a fuel fabrication plant in northern Saskatchewan. We will make our own CANDU fuel bundles.

Energy Independence

Fuel bundles power Saskatchewan’s own fleet of CANDU nuclear reactors, with excess fuel and electrons sold on the market. France and Europe, which are relying on nuclear power to transition away from Russian gas, will buy our fuel rods at a dignity-based price.

Medical Isotopes

CANDU reactors produce Molybdenum-99, used in cancer treatment and medical imaging worldwide. We will flood the market with these isotopes to bring the price down and help cure disease cheaply.

Trade Shield

CETA’s Article 28.6 contains a national security exception for fissionable materials. The nuclear vertical may be shielded from trade challenges. And because CANDU reactors use unenriched uranium, there is zero proliferation risk.

Policy 19

THE FACTORIES

The Saskatchewan Manufacturing Corporation is the engine that physically builds the new economy.

SMC-H1

Housing

A prefab modular housing factory operating 24/7 on four rotating shifts. Builds SaskHomes modules for 100,000 public housing units. Capacity: 5,000–10,000 units per year.

SMC-E1

Energy

Assembles heat pumps for SaskEnergy’s thermal utility conversion, hardware for the Virtual Power Plant, and commercial and grid-scale batteries. 50,000 heat pump units annually.

SMC-Ag

Agriculture

Six regional abattoirs, two flour mills, and a canola crushing plant. We process our own commodities in our own facilities. No more shipping wheat out to buy bread back.

SMC-N

Nuclear

Uranium conversion and fuel fabrication facilities in northern Saskatchewan. High-wage jobs with Indigenous hiring targets of at least 30%.

Every factory is union. Every factory is net-zero within 10 years. Every product carries a digital material passport. Every facility has multi-year contracts with Crown corporations, guaranteeing demand. The Crowns create the demand. The factories meet it. The workers are trained by the Century Corps and the unions.

Policy 14

The Food Chain

The Saskatchewan Agriculture Board puts the value chain back in the hands of farmers. Voluntary commodity pools with a Spring Time Advance of up to 75% of projected crop value upfront, interest-free. An independent Grading Arbitration Office. Bulk procurement at wholesale cost. A Farmland Trust with Right of First Refusal to stop Bay Street from buying up the land.

SaskGrocery operates at system-wide break-even. The Essentials Basket — bread, milk, eggs, flour, baby formula, seasonal produce — is sold at landed cost + logistics + 1%. The same price in Stony Rapids as in Moose Jaw. Fifty retail locations within five years, prioritizing food deserts.

Feed Saskatchewan First

Domestic processors and SaskGrocery get first right to purchase from SAB pools before export. Distribution via the revived Saskatchewan Transport Corporation using cold-storage buses.

The Potash Lever

Saskatchewan holds nearly a third of the world’s potash supply. A strategic reserve for potash, nitrogen, and finished fertilizer, sold cheap to our farmers at wholesale. Yet another lever in the gambit.

Policy 17

THE FINANCIAL FORTRESS

If the credit rating agencies get moody — and they might — the NCP has already built the walls.

The Bank

Saskatchewan Financial Group

Reshores the provincial float of daily transaction cash from the Big Five banks. Approximately $3 billion in operating balances moved over 36 months into a new Crown corporation. The SFG provides wholesale funding to credit unions at rates tied to provincial borrowing costs.

The Fund

Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund

A permanent, intergenerational endowment. Seeded with $500 million in Year 1. The principal is protected by supermajority lock. Annual interest is split by law: 54% to the Dignity Dividend, 15% to Reconciliation, 15% to Resilience and Energy Veterans, 10% to debt servicing, 5% to strategic investment, 1% to administration.

Even if the agencies lower our credit rating, it means little to the people of Saskatchewan. We have our own banking system. The SSF purchases the junior tranche of all SaskBonds, absorbing first-loss risk. The senior tranche sold to the public is backstopped by both the Sovereignty Fund and the full faith and credit of the Government of Saskatchewan.

Policies 05 & 06

The Housing Machine

A 5-year rent freeze gives immediate relief. Then 100,000 public housing units over 10–15 years, built by SMC-H1, ordered by the Saskatchewan Housing Corporation. Rent set at 30% of family income. Every unit is Passive House efficient, universally accessible, connected to SaskEnergy’s geothermal ambient loops, and prewired for solar and battery.

The Public REIT

Housing is built at cost — no developer profit margin. The rent pays the bond coupon. The Dignity Dividend ensures tenants can always pay. The Sovereignty Fund buys the junior tranche. The senior tranche, backed by proven rental revenue and government credit, targets a potential AA rating.

It pays out to Saskatchewan bondholders. It houses Saskatchewan people. And it is backed by the most stable possible revenue: rent that is protected by a universal basic income and capped at 30% of income.

Connected Communities

SaskEnergy becomes a thermal utility, laying 5th Generation Ambient Loops under city streets. SaskTel rolls out fibre to every household and farm. SaskPower modernizes the grid for EVs and extreme weather. Municipalities replace lead and asbestos pipes.

All coordinated under a “Dig Once” policy — one disruption, all the upgrades. Municipal bonds become extremely safe because the underlying infrastructure is brand new.

Policies 02, 08, 16 & 21

THE BACKSTOPS

Policy 02

The Dignity Dividend

$350 per month to every Saskatchewan resident. No questions asked. Indexed to CPI. The floor beneath every other policy. It ensures rent gets paid, groceries get bought, and people have breathing room to retrain, relocate, or start a co-op.

Policy 08

The Co-operative Backstop

If corporations flee, they cannot take the toys or the kids. The Right of Worker First Refusal Act gives workers 150 days to collectively bid on any closing business, backed by the Saskatchewan Co-operative Development Agency. Capital can leave. Skills, assets, and workers stay.

Policy 16

The Digital Fortress

Saskatchewan owns its own data. The Saskatchewan Digital Services builds a sovereign tech stack — unified login, unified data architecture, local encryption, all running on SaskTel’s secure cloud servers. No American-owned platforms holding Saskatchewan government data.

Policy 21

The Bonds

Everything is paid for upfront by selling SaskBonds. Six series: Foundation, Housing, Energy, Infrastructure, Manufacturing, Investment. Every bond is ring-fenced for its specific purpose. The bonds are backed by real resources and real rental payments — stable, physical-asset-backed instruments in a world starving for safe yield.

Policy 13

The Long Game

Within 36 months, the Saskatchewan Environmental Economics Agency will compile the most comprehensive accounting of climate damages ever assembled in a Canadian province. Healthcare costs. Emergency response. Infrastructure damage. Crop losses. Water treatment. Lost future GDP. Wrongful death. Treaty rights violations. Psychological injury. Charter violations.

The Government of Saskatchewan will sue the fossil fuel industry and the Government of Canada. The figure will be in the trillions.

The Lawsuit

Liability is established by market share of global emissions. The burden of proof shifts to the defendants. Statistical and climate-attribution evidence is explicitly admissible. Asset forfeiture for non-payment. Joint and several liability among all defendants. Financial institutions that funded extraction while knowing about climate risks may be named.

All lawsuit proceeds go directly into the Saskatchewan Sovereignty Fund.

The Ottawa Quid Pro Quo

The Government of Canada will be offered a clear arrangement: do not interfere with the full implementation of the S21P, and we will exclude the federal government from the Climate Crisis Lawsuit’s regulatory negligence claims.

Ottawa has made climate promises — the Paris Agreement, the carbon tax, the emission reduction targets. They have the data. They have the internal reports. A lawsuit using their own commitments against them would be the lawsuit of the century. Or they can let Saskatchewan do its thing. Their choice.

Legal Foundation

Research Notes

Tsilhqot’in Nation v. British Columbia (2014)

Established that Aboriginal title is a beneficial interest in the land itself. The Crown must obtain consent from the title-holding group before using the land. Strengthens the Indigenous Shield.

Yahey v. British Columbia (2021)

Found that the cumulative effects of industrial development infringed Treaty 8 rights. Establishes precedent for climate and environmental damages linked to Treaty rights violations.

Orphan Well Association v. Grant Thornton (Redwater, 2019)

Supreme Court of Canada ruled that provincial environmental cleanup obligations take priority over the claims of creditors in bankruptcy. Validates the Redwater Strategy’s legal foundation.

Natural Resources Transfer Act (1930)

Transferred control of Crown lands and natural resources to the prairie provinces. Saskatchewan’s constitutional authority over its resources is the bedrock of the entire platform.
